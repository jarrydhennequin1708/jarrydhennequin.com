Centrelink Service Design Accessibility

Modernising Social Services

Redesigned Centrelink's outdated portal using user-centered design and iterative sprints. Created an AAA-rated accessible design system, implemented debt prevention features, and overhauled the user interface.

Challenge

The Department of Human Services (DHS), managing Centrelink services for 7 million Australians, faced a critical issue: their online portal was outdated and inaccessible, leaving many vulnerable Australians behind. Key challenges included:

  1. An out-of-date online portal with poor usability, leading to confusion and frustration.
  2. A lack of WCAG 2.0 AAA compliance, excluding people with disabilities from accessing services online.
  3. Limited functionality resulting in high call centre volumes and Centrelink office visits.
  4. A fragmented user experience across the welfare system, leading to redundant data entry and errors.
  5. Inadequate debt prevention measures, causing unintended debts and financial hardship for recipients.

Approach

As part of a large cross-functional team, I applied user-centered design principles and iterative development to address these challenges:

  1. User Research and Empathy Mapping: I conducted interviews with caseworkers and service users to deeply understand their needs and pain points.
  2. Accessibility Focus: I designed all interfaces with WCAG 2.0 AAA compliance in mind, using high contrast colors, screen reader compatibility, and keyboard navigation.
  3. Iterative Design Sprints: I worked in two-week sprints, constantly validating designs through user testing and incorporating feedback.
  4. Data-Driven Decisions: I collaborated closely with data analysts to incorporate real-time income data, reducing errors and preventing debts.
  5. Cross-Agency Collaboration: I worked across government agencies to streamline information sharing, reducing redundancy for users.

Opportunities

Through this process, I identified and implemented several key opportunities:

  1. Accessible Design System: Created a comprehensive AAA-rated design system that could be reused across all DHS digital products, ensuring consistent accessibility.
  2. Debt Prevention Mechanism: Designed a real-time income verification feature that alerts users to potential overpayments before debts accrue.
  3. Streamlined Information Sharing: Developed interfaces that allow for secure data sharing across government agencies, reducing redundant data entry and errors.
  4. Self-Service Capabilities: Designed intuitive self-service features for common tasks, reducing the need for phone calls or office visits.

Outcomes and Impact

The redesigned Centrelink portal delivered significant improvements for both users and the agency:

  1. Enhanced Accessibility: Achieved WCAG 2.0 AAA compliance, ensuring all Australians, including those with disabilities, can access services online.
  2. Reduced Debt Incidence: The real-time income verification feature has helped prevent thousands of unintended debts, reducing financial hardship for recipients.
  3. Decreased Call Centre Volume: Improved self-service options led to a 15% reduction in calls to Centrelink's call centre.
  4. User Satisfaction Increase: Post-launch surveys showed a 22% increase in user satisfaction with the online portal.
  5. Scalable Design System: The accessible design system is now being used across other DHS digital products, ensuring consistent quality and accessibility.

This project demonstrates how user-centered design and a commitment to accessibility can transform government services, ensuring they work for everyone. By putting the needs of vulnerable Australians at the center of the design process, we were able to create a portal that not only meets compliance standards but genuinely improves people's lives.
